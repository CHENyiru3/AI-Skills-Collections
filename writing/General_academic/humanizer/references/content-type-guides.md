# Content-Type Guides

Use this file when genre should shape how much voice, compression, or informality
to add.

## Academic and technical prose

Priorities:

- keep formal register
- preserve cautious claims that are evidence-based
- remove puffery, vagueness, and passive clutter
- make agency and evidence clearer

Do:

- replace empty openings with direct statements
- link claims to data, methods, or citations
- preserve domain terminology

Do not:

- add humor, slang, or personality flourishes
- replace justified hedging with overstatement
- force contractions

## Emails and memos

Priorities:

- get to the point quickly
- make the ask clear
- sound like a person, not a template

Do:

- answer "why am I reading this?" in the first line
- use contractions when appropriate
- shorten ceremonial phrasing

Do not:

- leave the request buried at the end
- overformalize basic coordination

## Blog posts and essays

Priorities:

- stronger point of view
- more natural rhythm
- concrete detail before abstraction

Do:

- vary sentence length
- allow a visible stance
- use examples that feel observed, not generic

Do not:

- force quirkiness
- turn every paragraph into a miniature conclusion

## Marketing and product copy

Priorities:

- replace hype with specifics
- orient around reader benefit
- keep energy without sounding synthetic

Do:

- name the real problem
- use metrics or concrete outcomes when available
- speak directly to the user

Do not:

- stack superlatives
- describe ordinary features as revolutionary

## Documentation and explanatory prose

Priorities:

- clarity first
- natural but controlled tone
- precise sequencing

Do:

- simplify filler
- prefer direct instructions
- keep terminology consistent

Do not:

- add personality that obscures procedure
- oversmooth transitions that are already clear
